If you just need a tuner on your board tonight, buy a Boss TU-3. It's about $90, it's accurate, it's nearly indestructible, and it'll be working five minutes after it arrives. We're not going to pretend otherwise.

A DIY tuner kit is for a different reason. So before you spend $129 on a Q-Tune, here's an honest look at what you trade and what you get.

## What you give up

- **Instant gratification.** A finished pedal works out of the box. A kit asks for 15 to 30 minutes (solderless) or an afternoon (solder) first.
- **A couple of features you might miss.** The TC Electronic PolyTune can read all six strings at once. Q-Tune tunes one string at a time, like most tuners.
- **Brand familiarity.** A Boss or a Peterson on your board is a known quantity to anyone who looks down at it.

None of that is small. If those things matter most to you, buy the finished pedal. You won't regret it.

## What you get

- **A pedal you built.** This is the point. People tend to value things they made themselves more than identical store-bought versions. Every time you step on a Q-Tune, you know you put it together.
- **A tuner that keeps improving.** Q-Tune gets firmware updates over USB-C in your browser. New display styles and features show up after you've built it, for free. Most finished tuners ship as-is and never change.
- **A big, bright display.** A full-color 2.8-inch screen you can read on a dark stage, with several tuning styles to choose from.
- **Something that's yours.** Custom standby screens, set lists, and display styles out of the box, plus an [open-source SDK](https://github.com/joulupukki/q-tune-sdk) for building your own tuner displays and standby screens. It's a tuner you can extend, not just use.

And it's a good tuner on its own merits: fast, accurate, true or buffered bypass. A couple of our builders have put it on their boards in place of a Peterson StroboStomp.

## The money

Let's be straight about price. At $129, Q-Tune costs more than a Boss TU-3, and a lot more than a used one. You're not buying it to save money. You're paying for the build, the display, and the firmware that keeps it current. If the only thing you care about is the cheapest way to get in tune, a used Boss wins, and that's fine.

## So is it worth it?

It comes down to one question: do you want to build it?

If "I built this" means nothing to you, a DIY kit isn't worth your time or money, and we'd rather you knew that now. But if assembling your own gear sounds like a good way to spend an evening, you get two things at once: the build, and a tuner you'll keep using for years.

That's the trade. For the right person, it's worth it.
